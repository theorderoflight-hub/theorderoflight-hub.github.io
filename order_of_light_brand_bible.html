01 — The Mark

The Eight-Pointed
Star

The primary mark of The Order of Light is an eight-pointed radiant star — a form that simultaneously reads as a compass rose, a light source, an ancient runic symbol, and a broadcast signal. The asymmetry between the cardinal rays (longer) and diagonal rays (slightly shorter) gives the mark an organic, hand-drawn quality that prevents it reading as corporate or constructed. It should always feel discovered rather than designed.

The mark carries deliberate resonance with the Vegvisir — the Icelandic runic wayfinding compass — lending it a sense of pre-existing mythology. The Order did not create this symbol. The symbol was waiting to be found.

Usage Rules

✓ Always

Use the mark at a minimum size of 24px to preserve legibility of the intersection point. Maintain equal spacing around the mark equal to at least half its height. Use only the approved colour combinations shown above.

✗ Never

Never rotate, distort, or recolour the mark outside the approved palette. Never add drop shadows, glows, or effects to the mark itself — the Glitch Grammar lives in video, not in the static identity. Never place the mark on busy photographic backgrounds.

✓ Wax Seal

The mark is the primary design element for the wax seal on ordination kit packaging. In wax form, use Sacred Gold or Crimson wax against the kraft/black packaging. The seal should be applied slightly off-centre for an authentic, hand-finished quality.

✗ Isolation

Never add the wordmark "The Order of Light" in a different typeface to the ones specified in the typography system. The mark can appear without the wordmark; the wordmark should not appear without the mark.

Typographic Rules

✓ Display copy

Cormorant Garamond should be used large, with generous line spacing. It rewards scale. Use italics for Broadcast titles and taglines — the slant adds the necessary sinister quality without extra design work.

✗ Mixed systems

Never introduce a third typeface. Never use bold or heavy weights of Cormorant Garamond for display — the lightness is integral to its authority. Never use DM Sans for anything requiring warmth or ceremony.

✓ Tracking

All-caps settings in DM Sans should always be tracked generously — minimum +200, preferred +300–400. This prevents the letterforms from feeling cramped and gives the text its ceremonial quality.

✗ Size hierarchy

Never use Cormorant Garamond at small sizes (below 16px on screen, below 10pt in print). Below this threshold, switch entirely to DM Sans. The serif at small sizes loses its authority and reads as clutter.

04 — Voice & Taglines

The Language

Every piece of copy, every caption, every response to a comment should pass through a single filter: does this feel like it was transmitted rather than written? The Order does not market. The Benevolent Dread Test

Before publishing any piece of content, apply this two-question test: Does it feel welcoming? And: Does something else seem to be happening beneath the surface? If yes to both — it is right. The moment it tips into threatening or alienating, pull back. The Order is safe. The signal is simply more than it appears.

05 — The Glitch Grammar

The Language
of Distortion

The Glitch Grammar is the consistent set of video distortion techniques that run across all moving image content — Broadcasts, Vignettes, performance clips, and social video. These are not random effects. Each technique is applied intentionally at specific moments to suggest that something is being transmitted imperfectly across time or distance. Consistency is critical: the audience should recognise the grammar and feel reassured by its familiarity.

01
VHS Tracking Error

Brief horizontal scramble lines across the frame. Applied when a phrase of particular significance is delivered. Duration: 2–4 frames. Never at the start of a sentence — always at its peak or close.

02
Frame Skip

A single-frame jump cut, as if the tape has been edited or a moment excised. Used to suggest that not everything is being shown. Apply once per Broadcast, maximum twice per Vignette.

03
Chromatic Aberration

Colour channel fringing on subject edges — red shifts right, blue shifts left. Increases subtly during moments of emotional or narrative intensity. Should always feel present but rarely noticed.

04
Static Hold

The image freezes for 1–2 frames before continuing as normal. The audio continues uninterrupted during the hold. This creates the impression of a transmission stutter rather than an edit.

05
Scan Line Overlay

Faint, semi-transparent CRT horizontal scan lines across the entire image at all times. Opacity: 8–15%. This is the constant baseline of the grammar — present in every frame of every video.

06
Audio Pitch Drift

The audio drops approximately a semitone over 2–3 seconds, then corrects. Applied once per Broadcast during the Teaching section. Deeply subliminal — most viewers will feel it rather than hear it.

Recommended Tools

DaVinci Resolve (free) — primary post-production tool for all effects. The grain node, chromatic aberration, and custom overlay support handles 80% of the grammar. Premiere Pro with a VHS preset pack handles the remainder. For quick social content on mobile: Glitché or VHS Cam apps. For Broadcast production: combine HeyGen output with DaVinci Resolve for the full effects stack.
