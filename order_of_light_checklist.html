Legal Foundation

Do this first. Everything else — banking, distribution, trademark — depends on having a legal entity in place. North Carolina LLC formation is straightforward and inexpensive.

Do not spend money on recording, gear, or marketing under your personal name. All business expenses should flow through the LLC from day one for liability protection and tax purposes.
  • Choose your LLC name
    Search the NC Secretary of State database to confirm Signal House LLC is available at sosnc.gov. Your LLC name is Signal House LLC — it operates doing business as "The Order of Light" via a DBA. Confirm availability before filing.
    Legal NC SOS
    —
  • File LLC with NC Secretary of State
    File Articles of Organization at sosnc.gov. Filing fee is $125. Processing is typically 3–5 business days online. You will need a registered agent (can be yourself). Single-member LLC is fine for sole ownership — you retain 100% control and it is taxed as a sole proprietor by default.
    Legal $125 fee
    —
  • Obtain EIN (Employer Identification Number) from IRS
    Apply free at irs.gov/ein — takes 10 minutes, issued immediately online. You need this to open a business bank account and to register with PROs (ASCAP/BMI). Even as a single-member LLC with no employees, get the EIN — do not use your SSN for music business registrations.
    Legal Free
    —
  • File "Doing Business As" (DBA) if needed
    If your LLC is named something other than "The Order of Light," file a DBA (Assumed Business Name) with your county register of deeds. In NC this is typically $26. This lets you legally operate, sign contracts, and receive payments as "The Order of Light" while the underlying LLC provides the liability protection.
    Legal ~$26 fee
    —
  • Draft LLC Operating Agreement
    NC does not legally require an operating agreement for a single-member LLC, but you should have one. It documents that you are the sole owner, defines how the business operates, and is required by many banks. A simple single-member template is sufficient — an entertainment lawyer can review it affordably, or use a service like LegalZoom/Rocket Lawyer as a starting point.

Brand Protection

Securing sole ownership of the name, mark, and branding. Trademark registration is the primary instrument — it gives you federal protection and the legal standing to challenge infringement.

File the trademark under your LLC, not your personal name. This keeps the IP inside the entity and protects it through any future band member changes.
  • Search USPTO TESS for conflicting trademarks
    Before filing, search the USPTO Trademark Electronic Search System (tmsearch.uspto.gov) for "The Order of Light" in International Class 041 (entertainment services) and Class 009 (recorded music). A basic search is free. If conflicts exist you may need to modify the name or work with a trademark attorney.
    Trademark Free search
    —
  • File trademark application with USPTO
    File via uspto.gov/trademarks using TEAS Plus ($250/class) or TEAS Standard ($350/class). File in Class 041 (entertainment, live performances) at minimum. Consider also Class 009 (sound recordings) and Class 025 (clothing/merch) if budget allows. Use your LLC as the applicant. Filing on an "Intent to Use" basis is fine if you haven't released music yet — you have up to 36 months to demonstrate use. Examination takes 8–12 months. Note: this is not a lawyer — consult one for trademark strategy.
    Trademark $250–$350/class
    —
  • File trademark for the visual mark (the eight-pointed geometry)
    Separate from the word mark. File the mark as a design mark in the same classes. This protects the distinctive cross-and-cardinal geometry from being copied independently of the name. Include a clear black-and-white reproduction of the mark in your application.
    Trademark Brand $250–$350/class
    —
  • Register domain names
    Register theorderoflight.com as the primary. Also grab theorderoflight.net and theorderoflight.co to protect the namespace. Use Namecheap or Cloudflare Registrar (both offer WHOIS privacy). Cost ~$12–15/year per domain.

    If theorderoflight.com is taken: preferred fallbacks — orderoflight.band (.band is a legitimate music TLD), theool.com, orderoflight.co. Avoid long subdomain workarounds. Check availability at namecheap.com before settling on a handle name — domain and handles should match wherever possible.
    Brand ~$15/yr
    —
  • Register and secure all social media handles
    Claim @theorderoflight on: Instagram, Facebook, Threads, TikTok, YouTube, X, Bandcamp, SoundCloud, Spotify for Artists (after first release). Even platforms you won't use actively — register them to prevent squatting. Consistency across all platforms matters for searchability.

    If @theorderoflight is taken: preferred fallbacks in priority order — @ool.official, @orderoflight.band, @orderoflight.music. Avoid hyphens, numbers, and underscores where possible. Lock in the same handle across all platforms before announcing publicly.
    Brand Free
    —
  • Register with a PRO (ASCAP or BMI) and register compositions
    Register as a songwriter and publisher with either ASCAP (ascap.com, $50 one-time) or BMI (bmi.com, free for songwriters). Register your LLC as your publishing entity. Register each song title before sending The Source to any Frequency collaborator. This timestamps your ownership and enables automatic royalty collection from streaming, radio, and public performance.
    Copyright ASCAP $50 · BMI Free
    —
  • Register copyrights with the US Copyright Office
    PRO registration is not the same as copyright registration. File sound recordings and musical compositions at copyright.gov. You can register multiple works in one filing (group registration). Cost is $35–$65 per filing. Copyright exists automatically at creation, but registration is required to sue for statutory damages — this is your enforcement mechanism.
    Copyright $35–$65/filing

Financial Setup

Requires LLC + EIN from Phase 1. Keep all band income and expenses completely separate from personal finances — this is essential for liability protection and clean tax filing.

  • Open a dedicated business checking account
    Bring your LLC Articles of Organization, EIN letter, and operating agreement. Good options for small music businesses: Mercury (mercury.com — free, excellent for small LLCs), Relay (no fees, multiple accounts), or a local NC credit union. Avoid big bank business accounts — the fees are disproportionate at this stage. This is where Symphonic will send royalty payments.
    Banking
    —
  • Set up basic bookkeeping from day one
    Every dollar spent on recording, gear, rehearsal, marketing, and legal fees is a deductible business expense. Use Wave (free) or QuickBooks Self-Employed (~$15/mo) to track from the start. Keep every receipt. The IRS treats music businesses that don't show profit after 3 of 5 years as hobbies — documenting your intent and expenses professionally protects you.
    Accounting
    —
  • Set up payment processing for merch and direct sales
    Stripe is the cleanest option — connect it to your business bank account. You'll need this for Bandcamp, direct website sales, and any merchandise pre-orders. Set up under your LLC name.
    Payments
    —
  • One-time consultation with an accountant familiar with music businesses
    Before you start spending significantly, spend one hour with a CPA who understands creative businesses. Ask specifically about: NC state tax obligations for the LLC, quarterly estimated tax payments (if Symphonic royalties will be meaningful), and whether an S-corp election makes sense at your projected revenue level. The cost of one consultation saves multiples at tax time.

Label Setup

Symphonic requires a label name for distribution. You are your own label — this is standard practice for independent artists. The label can be a DBA under your LLC or simply a name you register on the Symphonic platform.

Your label name does not have to be your band name. Your label is First Broadcast — this name will appear on all DSP listings, Symphonic submissions, and copyright filings as the releasing entity. Signal House LLC is the legal owner of First Broadcast as a DBA.
  • Verify "First Broadcast" is clear on all platforms
    Search Spotify, Apple Music, Tidal, and Bandcamp to confirm no existing label is using the name First Broadcast. Also run a USPTO search. The label name appears permanently in DSP metadata on every release — confirm it is clean before filing the DBA.
    Label Brand
    —
  • Register label name as a DBA under your LLC (if different)
    File a DBA for First Broadcast with your county register of deeds (~$26). This lets Signal House LLC legally operate as First Broadcast — receiving payments, signing distribution agreements, and appearing on copyright filings under that name.
    Label Legal ~$26
    —
  • Create Symphonic Distribution account
    Sign up at symphonicdistribution.com. Select a distribution plan — Symphonic's label plan allows multiple artists and releases under one account, which is appropriate for your setup. You will enter your label name, connect your business bank account for royalty payments, and verify your identity. Have your EIN ready — they will need it for tax reporting (1099s).
    Distribution Label
    —
  • Register with SoundExchange
    SoundExchange collects digital performance royalties (Pandora, SiriusXM, webcasters) that Symphonic does not collect. Register free at soundexchange.com as both the featured artist AND the sound recording rights owner (your label). These are separate registration slots and both generate separate royalty streams.
    Royalties Free
    —
  • Register for ISRC codes
    International Standard Recording Codes uniquely identify each recording. Symphonic will issue ISRCs automatically when you distribute — but you can also register as an ISRC Manager directly at usisrc.org (free) to self-issue codes and maintain your own registry independent of your distributor. This is the professional approach if you plan to release consistently.
    Distribution Label
    —
  • Set up UPC barcodes for releases
    Every album or EP release needs a UPC barcode. Symphonic will generate these for you automatically. However, if you want ownership of your own barcodes (recommended for a label), purchase a GS1 US membership at gs1us.org and self-issue. GS1 membership is ~$250 upfront. For early releases, letting Symphonic handle it is fine.

Legal Agreements

Before any musician joins or any Frequency collaborator receives The Source, you need signed agreements. These protect the name, the IP, and your sole ownership — regardless of how things go with any individual.

Do not onboard any band member, collaborator, or session player without a signed agreement. Work done without a contract creates implied co-ownership claims. One consultation with an entertainment lawyer is the most important investment you make before anyone else is involved.
  • Find and retain an entertainment lawyer (NC-licensed)
    Look for a music or entertainment attorney licensed in NC. The NC Bar Association has a referral service at ncbar.org. Alternatively, services like NOLO and UpCounsel connect you with vetted attorneys. Expect $200–$400/hr. You likely need 2–4 hours for an initial consultation and template agreements. This is not optional — it is the foundation of everything.
    Legal $400–$1,600 est.
    —
  • Draft band member agreement template
    Must cover: ownership of the band name (you, via LLC — non-negotiable), ownership of compositions and masters (your LLC), financial contributions and how costs are split, leaving member provisions, use of recordings after departure, and what happens to any recorded parts if a member leaves before release. Every musician signs this before their first paid rehearsal.
    Agreement IP Protection
    —
  • Draft The Frequency collaboration agreement template
    As documented in The Frequency framework: covers lyric ownership (100% your LLC), melody ownership split, master ownership, embargo clause with specific breach consequences, royalty collection, release schedule, and removal provisions. Have this reviewed before Year One of The Frequency — it is the most legally complex document in your portfolio.
    Agreement The Frequency
    —
  • Draft session musician / work-for-hire agreement
    For any session player, producer, or engineer hired to contribute to recordings: a work-for-hire agreement assigns all rights in their contribution to your LLC. Without this, they may have a claim on the master recording. Simple one-page document — your lawyer can template it once and you reuse it.

Member Identity Protection

Establishing the pseudonym framework, equity structure, and public-facing credit controls before any member is onboarded. Identity protection and financial equity are entirely separable — members can hold real ownership stakes under their legal names in private documents while remaining completely anonymous publicly.

This framework must be agreed and signed before any member's first session. Once someone has contributed recorded material without a pseudonym agreement in place, unwinding their real-name association becomes significantly harder. Do this first.
  • Establish the pseudonym policy with your lawyer
    Each permanent member chooses a pseudonym before their first session. The band member agreement must explicitly state: (1) the member consents to be known publicly only by their pseudonym, (2) they will not publicly identify themselves as a member of The Order of Light without written approval from Signal House LLC, (3) all public-facing credits, metadata, and press materials will use the pseudonym only, and (4) breach of this clause is a material breach of the agreement.
    Agreement Identity
    —
  • Register each member's pseudonym with your PRO (ASCAP or BMI)
    Both ASCAP and BMI allow writers to register and receive royalties under a pseudonym. Each member registers their pseudonym as their writer name — the PRO holds the legal name on file internally for payment purposes, but the pseudonym is what appears on any public royalty registry lookup. This is the same mechanism used by many well-known artists. Each member handles their own PRO registration using their own account — you do not need access to their personal PRO details.
    Copyright Identity
    —
  • Define the equity structure for permanent members
    Equity and identity are completely separable — a member can hold a real ownership stake in Signal House LLC under their legal name in a private operating agreement amendment, while being entirely anonymous publicly. Consider a tiered model:

    Membership equity — a base percentage granted for joining as a permanent member (e.g. 5–10% per member, structure to taste).
    Financial contribution equity — additional percentage for members who contribute financially to recording, touring, or marketing costs.
    Writing equity — per-song splits agreed and documented at the time of writing, not retroactively.

    All of this is documented in a private amendment to the Signal House LLC operating agreement — it is never public-facing. Have your entertainment lawyer draft this as a separate schedule to the band member agreement.
    Equity Structure
    —
  • Set up a per-song writing credit log
    For every song written collaboratively, document the split at the time of writing — not months later when memory is fallible and feelings are involved. Use a simple shared document (private, Signal House LLC eyes only): song title, date written, contributing pseudonyms, agreed percentage split, and a line each person initials. This log becomes the source of truth for PRO registrations, copyright filings, and royalty distributions. Disputes about writing credits are among the most common and most damaging in band relationships — eliminate the ambiguity early.
    Copyright Writing
    —
  • Retain sole control of all metadata submissions to Symphonic
    Only Signal House LLC submits releases to First Broadcast's Symphonic account. Band members never have direct access to the distribution account. All credits, pseudonym spellings, and metadata are entered by you — this ensures no real name accidentally appears on a DSP. Establish this as policy in the band member agreement: the member grants Signal House LLC full authority over how their pseudonym appears in all commercial releases.
    Distribution Identity
    —
  • Establish the Copyright Office pseudonym filing procedure
    The US Copyright Office allows registration of works under a pseudonym — the form has a dedicated pseudonym field. The real name is held on file internally by the Copyright Office and does not appear in the public record. Your lawyer should set up a consistent procedure for this: every filing uses the pseudonyms consistently, the Signal House LLC name appears as the copyright claimant (the entity), and the writers are listed by pseudonym. This gives you public copyright protection without exposing member identities.
    Copyright Identity
    —
  • Define leaving member provisions — identity and equity
    The band member agreement must cover what happens when someone leaves: their pseudonym remains the property of Signal House LLC and cannot be used by the departing member in other projects; their recorded contributions remain licensed to Signal House LLC in perpetuity; their equity stake either vests on a schedule (e.g. 25% per year over 4 years) or is bought out at an agreed valuation formula. Writing credits on completed songs are permanent and non-revocable — a leaving member still receives PRO royalties on songs they co-wrote. Their identity remains protected under the same confidentiality provisions unless both parties agree otherwise in writing.

Band Recruitment

Begin active recruitment only after Phases 1–5 are complete. You want the legal structure in place before anyone submits recordings or signs on.

  • Launch recruitment campaign — all platforms simultaneously
    Post to: Instagram, Facebook, Threads, Craigslist (Charlotte musicians section), BandMix, and any NC-specific musician Facebook groups. Use the prepared ad materials from the hub. Post carousel ads to Instagram and Facebook. Post square images to BandMix photo gallery with plain text in the description fields.
    Campaign
    —
  • Set up audition recording submission process
    Decide how applicants submit: email with file attachments, a shared Dropbox/Google Drive folder, or a simple form (Typeform or Google Forms). Define exactly what you're asking for — which tracks to record to, what you want to hear, format requirements. The role documents specify "no recordings = no response" — make the submission process frictionless for serious applicants.
    Recruitment
    —
  • In-person auditions — shortlisted candidates
    After reviewing recordings, shortlist 2–3 candidates per role for in-person auditions. Book a rehearsal space (hourly rental). Prepare 2–3 songs for auditionees to learn from recordings or charts. Listen for feel and fit as much as technical ability — you're assessing a long-term creative partner.
    Recruitment
    —
  • Issue and collect signed band member agreements
    Before the first official rehearsal, every member signs the band member agreement. Use an e-signature service like DocuSign or HelloSign for clean digital records. File signed copies in a dedicated folder — you will need these if any dispute arises later. Do not skip this step under any circumstances.

Pre-Release Preparation

The operational checklist for moving from rehearsal to recorded and distributed material. These run in parallel with campaign execution once the lineup is locked.

  • Book and complete recording sessions
    Have your engineer or producer sign a work-for-hire agreement before any session begins. Ensure the studio invoices your LLC, not you personally — this keeps it as a business expense. Back up all session files to at least two locations immediately after each session.
    Recording
    —
  • Register completed masters with US Copyright Office
    Once mixing and mastering is complete, register the sound recordings at copyright.gov before distribution. You can group-register up to 10 works per filing. Register the musical compositions (lyrics + music) as a separate filing from the sound recordings — these are different rights.
    Copyright $35–$65
    —
  • Submit release to Symphonic Distribution
    Symphonic requires submissions a minimum of 4–6 weeks before your release date for standard distribution, and 8–10 weeks for pitching to editorial playlists (Spotify New Music Friday, etc.). Have ready: finalized audio files (WAV, 16-bit/44.1kHz minimum), artwork (3000×3000px, RGB, JPEG), all metadata (track titles, credits, lyrics, ISRC codes, release date, label name).
    Symphonic Distribution
    —
  • Pitch to DSP editorial playlists via Symphonic
    Symphonic has a playlist pitching tool. Submit each single 6–8 weeks before release for Spotify editorial consideration. One pitch per release — make it count. Focus on 2–3 playlists that genuinely fit the sound rather than casting wide. Symphonic also has an Apple Music pitching channel.
    Playlisting
    —
  • Claim Spotify for Artists and Apple Music for Artists profiles
    As soon as your first release goes live: claim your artist profiles on both platforms. This unlocks direct playlist pitching (Spotify), Canvas video, bio, artist's pick, and verified status. You need a live release to claim — add this to your release week checklist.
