01 — The Concept

The same signal.
Three entirely
different receptions.

Every year, The Order of Light writes a single song and distributes it — incompletely, deliberately — to two collaborating artists. Each artist receives exactly the same material: a title, a set of lyrics, and a Nashville Numbers chord chart with a note of the original key. Nothing else. No demo. No reference track. No communication with each other or with the band.

Each artist builds their own version from that raw material alone. They choose their key. They write their own melody. They make every production decision in a vacuum. The only thing they share with the other artists is the source.

The Order of Light's version drops first. The collaborators follow, one per week. The world hears the same song three times — and three entirely different songs each time.

"We sent them the signal. What they received — that was always going to be theirs."

Transmission

Each released interpretation — The Order of Light's, the established artist's, the emerging artist's — is a Transmission. The same word used for the Sermons. The mythology holds. The signal continues.

Why This Works

The creative tension at the heart of The Frequency is the tension between sameness and difference. Three artists start at exactly the same coordinate and end up in completely different places. That is both a compelling artistic experiment and a genuinely press-worthy story — the kind of concept that a music journalist can explain in one sentence and spend three paragraphs exploring.

The staggered release turns a single song into a three-week event. Each Transmission drives curiosity backwards — listeners who discover the established artist's version go looking for the original. Listeners who find the emerging artist's version discover both the band and the established collaborator. Every version is a door into the others.

Across years, The Frequency becomes an archive. By year five, there are fifteen versions of five different songs in existence. That is an extraordinary body of work built entirely around a single annual ritual.

02 — The Rules

What every collaborator
receives. And what
they never do.

The integrity of The Frequency depends entirely on these rules being observed without exception. They are not suggestions. They are the mechanism that makes the concept work — and the first time they are broken, the concept is broken with them.

1
The Source — and nothing else

Each collaborating artist receives The Source document: the song title, the complete lyrics, and the Nashville Numbers chord chart with the original key noted for reference only. This is the complete material package. No demo, no reference recording, no voice memo, no communication about feel, tempo, or vibe. The source is the source.

2
No communication between collaborators

The collaborating artists must not communicate with each other about the song, their interpretation, their approach, or any creative element of their version. The Order of Light does not facilitate introductions until after all versions are released. If collaborators know each other personally, they are asked to treat this as a condition of participation.

3
The melody belongs to each artist

Because no original melody is shared, each artist is free — and expected — to write their own melodic interpretation of the lyrics. The melody they create is their creative contribution. The Order of Light's original melody is revealed only when its Transmission drops. No artist hears another's melody before release.

4
The key is a suggestion, not an instruction

The original key is noted on The Source for reference only. Each artist chooses their own key. The Nashville Numbers format exists precisely to make the chart playable in any key without the chart changing. An artist who records in a different key than the original is not departing from the concept — they are enacting it.

5
The embargo holds until release

No artist — including The Order of Light — releases, previews, leaks, teases, or discusses the musical content of their version before its scheduled release date. Social posts, lyric drops, and pre-release communication are coordinated centrally. The reveal is the concept. The embargo protects the reveal.

6
The lyrics are not altered

Each artist performs the lyrics as provided. Structural reordering of verses and choruses may be permitted at the discretion of The Order of Light, but substantive lyrical changes are not. The lyrics are the one constant across all three Transmissions — the thread connecting versions that are otherwise entirely distinct.

7
Each version stands alone

There is no definitive version. The Order of Light's Transmission is not the original against which others are measured — it is the first to be heard. All three Transmissions are presented as equal, complete interpretations of the same source material. This framing must be consistent across all press and promotional communication.

03 — Release Strategy

A three-week event.
One song.
Three arrivals.

The staggered release is not just a marketing decision — it is part of the artistic structure. Each Transmission needs space to be heard on its own terms before the next one recontextualises it. A simultaneous drop would collapse that space. The weekly cadence creates anticipation, comparison, and conversation across three weeks rather than one afternoon.

Week One — The Order of Light
Transmission I — The Band's Version

The Order of Light's interpretation drops first. This is the version against which the others will be measured — not as the original (framing matters) but as the first. The announcement reveals the concept of The Frequency for the first time. Listeners are told that two further Transmissions are coming. No names yet. The concept itself is the hook.

Week Two — The Established Artist
Transmission II — The Known Name

The established collaborator's version drops seven days after the first. Their audience discovers The Order of Light. The Order of Light's existing audience discovers the collaborator's version. The contrast between the two interpretations — already audible — becomes the conversation. The third Transmission is now announced but unreleased.

Week Three — The Emerging Artist
Transmission III — The New Voice

The emerging artist's version completes the trilogy. This is often the most surprising Transmission — the one with the least precedent and the most freedom. Their platform is expanded by proximity to both previous releases. All three versions are now in the world simultaneously.

Week Four — The Reveal
All Three Artists Together

A single post or short-form video featuring all three artists reacting to each other's versions for the first time. The moment of genuine, unscripted discovery — hearing another artist's interpretation of your song — is real content. Film it. Release it. This is the most human moment in the entire series.

Collaborator Selection Strategy

The pairing of one established artist with one emerging artist is not an arbitrary structure — it is a deliberate value exchange. The established name brings an existing audience and immediate credibility. The emerging artist brings freshness, gratitude, and often the most unexpected interpretation. Together they create a contrast that the established artist alone could not.

  • The established artist should ideally share sonic territory with The Order of Light without being identical to it — a name that makes sense alongside the band but surprises no one. Their team will want a collaboration agreement in place before recording begins
  • The emerging artist should be genuinely talented and genuinely hungry — someone for whom this platform matters and who will bring their full creative investment to the interpretation. The Order of Light picks the emerging artist; the emerging artist does not audition
  • Genre divergence is a feature — a collaborator from outside the immediate sonic territory of The Order of Light produces a more interesting contrast than one who sounds adjacent. The concept handles the connective tissue. The lyrics are the same. That is enough
  • Personal connection is fine — working with artists you know and trust removes friction. The concept doesn't require strangers. It requires isolation during the creation process, not before or after

Social Media & Press Narrative

  • Announce The Frequency as a series concept alongside Transmission I — not just as a single release
  • The press hook is the concept, not the song: "We sent them a piece of paper. We never spoke again until release day."
  • Short documentary content — each artist talking about receiving The Source, their process, their choices — is the ideal pre-release content for Transmissions II and III
  • The Week Four reaction content is potentially the highest-performing piece of the entire series. Prioritise filming it
  • All three versions should be pitched to playlists simultaneously after Week Three — "The Frequency: Complete" as a playlist or bundle is a natural editorial pitch
04 — The Nashville Number System

How The Source
stays keyless.

The Nashville Number System is a method of writing chord charts using numerals instead of letter names — each number represents a scale degree relative to whatever key the song is in. A chart written in Nashville Numbers can be played in any key without any changes to the chart itself. This is exactly why it is the right format for The Source.

By providing a Nashville Numbers chart with the original key noted only as a reference point, The Order of Light gives collaborating artists a complete harmonic map of the song that belongs to no key until they choose one. The same chart that produced the original version in G major becomes a different song when an artist plays it in Bb, or D minor, or wherever their voice and instincts take them.

How to Notate The Source Chart

Common notation conventions used in The Source document:

  • Plain number (1, 4, 5) — major chord on that scale degree
  • Minus sign (6-, 2-) — minor chord on that scale degree
  • Triangle (1△) — major seventh chord
  • Number with a 7 (5⁷) — dominant seventh chord
  • Diamond (◇) — let ring / whole note duration
  • Bars and slashes — standard bar lines and beat divisions
  • Repeat marks — used for verse/chorus repetitions
Example Source Chart Format
Key of E (reference only — choose your own)
[Verse]
| 1 . . . | 6- . . . | 4 . . . | 5 . . . |
| 1 . . . | 6- . . . | 4 . . . | 4 . . . |
[Pre-Chorus]
| 4 . . . | 5 . . . | 6- . . . | 5 . . . |
[Chorus]
| 1 . . . | 4 . . . | 5 . . . | 6- . . . | x2
[Bridge]
| 4△ . . . | 4△ . . . | 1 . . . | 5 . . . |

The chart above gives a collaborating artist everything they need to understand the harmonic structure of the song while leaving melody, key, tempo, feel, arrangement, and production entirely open. Two artists handed this chart in a room with no further instruction will produce two different songs. That is the point.

05 — Business, Royalties & Legal

Being fair.
Not being naive.

The Frequency involves real intellectual property, real recordings, and real revenue potential. These things need to be handled correctly — both to protect The Order of Light's ownership of what it has created and to treat collaborators fairly. The following framework is a starting point, not a legal document. Run the final agreements past an entertainment lawyer before Year One.

What The Order of Light Always Retains

  • 100% of lyrical copyright — across all three Transmissions, in perpetuity. The lyrics are not negotiable. They are provided to collaborators under a licence, not a transfer. Register them with your PRO (ASCAP or BMI) before sending The Source to anyone
  • 100% of composition and master for the OOL Transmission — your version is entirely yours. No split of any kind on the version you record
  • The Source as a document — the chart, the title, and the lyrics as a combined work belong to The Order of Light

Royalty Split Models — Collaborator Versions

Because no original melody is provided and collaborators write their own melodic interpretation, they have a legitimate creative contribution to the composition of their version. The split model you use depends on the collaborator and the context.

Important — Read Before Year One

The splits above are a framework, not legal advice. An entertainment lawyer should draft or review the collaboration agreement before you approach any artist. The agreement needs to cover: what rights are being licensed, what the collaborator can and cannot do with the material, the embargo clause and its consequences, royalty collection and distribution, and what happens if a collaborator wants to remove their version from streaming. This is not expensive to do correctly and it is very expensive to fix after the fact.

Register your lyrics and composition with your PRO — ASCAP or BMI — before sending The Source to anyone. Once the lyrics leave your hands, the registration is your proof of prior ownership.

The Embargo Clause — The Most Important Piece of Paper

The embargo clause in the collaboration agreement must specify: the earliest date on which the collaborator may release, discuss, preview, or reference their Transmission in any public forum; that breach of the embargo is a material breach of the agreement; and what the remedy is if a breach occurs.

The reveal mechanic — listeners discovering three versions of the same song in sequence, never knowing what the next one sounds like — is the entire creative and commercial engine of The Frequency. A single leak kills it for that year. The embargo clause is not a formality. It is the structural foundation the whole concept rests on. Treat it accordingly.

Practical Administration

  • Register lyrics with your PRO before sending The Source — ASCAP or BMI. One registration, done before any collaboration begins
  • Use DistroKid, TuneCore or your existing distributor for each Transmission — each release is a separate single. Set publishing splits correctly at the point of distribution for each version
  • Collaborators distribute their own Transmissions — they own their masters, they handle their own distribution. You handle yours
  • Collect your composition royalties through your PRO — this happens automatically once songs are registered and generating streams. Make sure your PRO registration includes the correct splits for each version
  • One agreement per collaborator, per year — do not reuse agreements year to year without review. Terms may need to evolve
